Application of Derivatives · Extrema of Integral-Defined Functions

An integral is fullest exactly where its integrand turns from plus to minus.

Picture the tank on your terrace. Every minute, water either flows in or drains out, at some rate that keeps changing. Call that rate f(t). The amount of water standing in the tank after x minutes is the running total of that rate — an integral. On this page you will find the moment the tank is fullest, and the moment it is emptiest, without ever computing how much water is inside. You only watch the rate.

01The idea, in depth

The concept

Fix a continuous rate f and a starting minute a. The water stored by minute x is

(1)

F(x) = ∫ax f(t) dt

F the accumulated total (litres) · f the rate the total grows (litres/min) · a the fixed start minute · x the present minute.

You want the minute at which F is largest — a local maximum — and the minute it is smallest. Ordinarily you would find a maximum of F by differentiating F. Here something lovely happens: differentiating an integral gives you the integrand straight back. That is the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, and it is the whole engine of this page.

★ (2)

FTC  F′(x) = f(x)

Valid when f is continuous. The derivative of the running total is the rate itself — the slope of F at minute x is the rate f(x) flowing at that minute.

Read eq. (2) as a sentence about the tank. When the rate is positive, water is flowing in, so the total climbs. When the rate is negative, water is draining, so the total falls. The instant the rate crosses from positive to negative, the tank stops filling and starts emptying — that is the moment it holds the most. The instant the rate crosses from negative to positive, the tank stops emptying and starts filling — the moment it holds the least.

So the extrema of F are decided entirely by the sign changes of the integrand. You never integrate. You watch where f is zero and which way it crosses.

Two things follow at once, and both matter for exams. First, the peak of F is not where f is largest — it is where f is zero and dropping. Confusing the total with its rate is the single most common blunder here. Second, the start minute a never appears in eq. (2). Changing a pours a fixed extra amount into the tank at time zero; it shifts every value of F by the same constant, but it cannot move the minute at which the tank is fullest. The locations of the extrema are blind to a.

Quick check

Let F(x) = ∫5x f(t) dt with f continuous. Then F′(x) equals —

Ans: f(x). By FTC, eq. (2) — the lower limit 5 leaves no trace in the derivative.

02Every symbol, and the one rule of sign

Before any calculation, pin down the quantities and the reading of "plus to minus". Everything below is decided by these.

F(x)
Integral-defined function

The running total ∫ax f. Its graph is the accumulation — the tank's water level over time.

f(t)
Integrand

The rate that F grows. Continuous, so FTC applies. Its sign is the only thing you study.

F′(x)
Derivative of the total

Equals f(x) exactly, by eq. (2). A critical point of F is a zero of f.

F″(x)
Second derivative

Equals f′(x). Its sign at a critical point classifies max versus min without a sign line.

03Deriving the two tools

Nothing above should be taken on faith. Here is FTC from the definition of the derivative, then the Leibniz rule that handles a limit which is itself a function of x.

Tool one — the derivative of a running total

1Write F′ straight from the definition of a derivative.

F′(x) = limh→0 [ F(x+h) − F(x) ] / h

2Subtract the two integrals; the shared part over [a, x] cancels.

F(x+h) − F(x) = ∫xx+h f(t) dt

3f is continuous, so on the thin strip [x, x+h] it barely moves from f(x).

∫xx+h f(t) dt = f(c) · h ,   c between x and x+h

4Divide by h and let h → 0, so c → x.

F′(x) = limh→0 f(c) = f(x)

∴The slope of the total is the rate itself.

F′(x) = f(x)

Step 3 is the mean-value theorem for integrals: the signed area of a thin slice equals the height at some inner point times the width. As the slice shrinks, that inner point is squeezed onto x. That is all FTC is.

Tool two — when the limit is a function of x

Often the upper limit is not x but something like x², and the lower limit may move too. Compose FTC with the chain rule.

1Name the "total up to u" function; its derivative is f by eq. (2).

H(u) = ∫au f(t) dt ,   H′(u) = f(u)

2A varying upper limit v(x) means H(v(x)); apply the chain rule.

d/dx H(v(x)) = f(v(x)) · v′(x)

3A varying lower limit enters with a minus (swap the limits).

g(x) = ∫u(x)v(x) f = H(v(x)) − H(u(x))

∴Differentiate both pieces — the Newton–Leibnitz rule.

g′(x) = f(v(x)) v′(x) − f(u(x)) u′(x)

★ (3)

Leibniz  g′(x) = f(v(x)) v′(x) − f(u(x)) u′(x)

v(x) upper limit · u(x) lower limit · each integrand value is multiplied by the derivative of its own limit. Take a constant limit's derivative as 0.

Notice what eq. (3) quietly introduces: the factor v′(x). A varying limit can create a critical point where v′(x)=0, even though the integrand is nowhere zero there. That extra root is where most marks are lost, and we hunt it down in the second scenario.

04The special cases, named

Four situations cover every question this subheading asks. Each is one line of trigger.

Constant lower limit  trigger: F = ∫ from a fixed a to x

Then F′(x) = f(x). Extrema live at the sign changes of f, and moving a only slides the whole graph of F up or down — the extrema stay put.

Function upper limit  trigger: upper limit is v(x), e.g. x²

Then g′(x) = f(v(x))·v′(x). Set both factors to zero: candidates come from f(v(x))=0 and from v′(x)=0.

Both limits vary  trigger: g = ∫ from u(x) to v(x)

The full rule g′ = f(v)v′ − f(u)u′. Factor it completely, then draw one sign line for g′.

Second-derivative test  trigger: classify a critical point fast

Since F″ = f′, at a zero c of f: if f′(c) < 0 it is a maximum, if f′(c) > 0 a minimum. If f′(c)=0 too, fall back to the sign line.

Key idea

Whatever the limits, first write g′(x) and factor it. The zeros of that factored form are your only candidates; a sign line across them, read left to right, hands you every maximum and minimum in one pass.

Quick check — type a number

For g(x)=∫0x² (t−1) dt, compute g′(1).

Ans: 0. By eq. (3), g′(x)=(x²−1)·2x, so g′(1)=0·2=0. The lab's preset "Composite limit x²" shows this live.

05Two worked scenarios

SCENARIO · AOD-032PYQ pattern · JEE Main

A parabola integrand — find the peak and the trough

The setup

Let F(x) = ∫0x (t−1)(t−2) dt on all of ℝ. Locate the local maximum and local minimum of F.

How to attack

1Do not integrate — FTC hands you the derivative directly.

F′(x) = (x−1)(x−2)

2Set it to zero for the candidate minutes.

(x−1)(x−2) = 0 ⟹ x = 1, 2

3Read the sign of F′ across the roots (FIG 3): + , − , +.

F′ > 0 on (−∞,1), < 0 on (1,2), > 0 on (2,∞)

∴+ → − at 1 is a peak; − → + at 2 is a trough.

local max at x = 1,  local min at x = 2

The trap

Students expand the bracket, integrate to F(x)=x³/3 − 3x²/2 + 2x, then differentiate it back — burning time and inviting a sign slip. Others report x=2 as the maximum because f is climbing there. Neither is needed, and the second is wrong.

The line that finishes it

F′ is the integrand itself; its + → − crossing is the maximum. Answer: max at x = 1, min at x = 2.

Quick check — type a number

For F(x)=∫0x(t−1)(t−2)dt, at which x is F a local maximum?

Ans: x = 1, where the integrand crosses + to −.

SCENARIO · AOD-033PYQ pattern · JEE Advanced

A composite upper limit — mind the extra root

The setup

Let g(x) = ∫0x² (t−1) dt on ℝ. Find every local maximum and minimum of g.

How to attack

1Apply Leibniz: value at the upper limit, times the limit's derivative.

g′(x) = (x²−1) · 2x

2Factor fully — three roots, not one.

g′(x) = 2x(x−1)(x+1) = 0 ⟹ x = −1, 0, 1

3Sign line across the three roots (FIG 4): − , + , − , +.

g′ < 0, > 0, < 0, > 0

∴Read the crossings: − → + minima, + → − maximum.

min at x = ±1,  max at x = 0

The trap

The costly mistake is writing g′(x)=x²−1 and forgetting the 2x from the chain rule — which drops the whole x=0 extremum. A quick sanity check: g(x)=x⁴/2 − x², so g′=2x³−2x=2x(x²−1), confirming the extra root.

The line that finishes it

Every varying limit brings its own derivative as a factor. Here that factor is 2x — so x = 0 is a genuine maximum, and the answer is min at ±1, max at 0.
