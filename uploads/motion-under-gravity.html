U02 · KINEMATICS — MOTION UNDER GRAVITY KIN-006 – KIN-011 · 6 CONCEPTS

Up and down are mirror images

Throw a ball straight up. Gravity pulls it the same way through the whole flight — the same pull while going up, at the top, and while coming down. Once you fix your sign convention and trust this symmetry, every question of this section — graphs, tower throws, two stones, even a fall broken into two parts — becomes the same short calculation.

01 Basic theory

What actually happens when something moves up or down near the Earth.

Near the Earth's surface, every object in free flight feels one and only one thing: a constant pull towards the ground. We call its acceleration g = 9.80 m/s², and it does not care whether the object is a cricket ball or an iron ball, whether it is rising or falling. Air resistance is left out of this model, and the whole motion stays on one vertical line.

FIG 1 — one throw, three moments. Velocity (cyan) keeps changing; the pull of gravity (orange) never does.

Look at the middle of Fig 1 carefully — this is the heart of the whole section. At the highest point the ball is momentarily at rest, but gravity is still pulling it with full strength. The velocity becomes zero; the acceleration never does. Most wrong answers in this chapter are born exactly here.

02 Definitions

Fix these five symbols and one convention before touching any formula.

u

Launch velocity — velocity at t = 0, in m/s. Positive when thrown upward.

v

Velocity at time t — in m/s. Positive while rising, zero at the top, negative while falling.

g

Acceleration due to gravity — 9.80 m/s², always directed downward, same for every mass.

y

Height above launch point — in metres. Negative when the object is below the point of throw.

t

Time from launch — in seconds.

+

Sign convention — take upward as positive. Then acceleration is −g everywhere: rising, top, falling.

FIX THE CONVENTION FIRST

Half the marks lost in this chapter are sign mistakes. Write "up = positive, a = −g" at the top of your rough work — then every equation below works without any special case.

03 Derivation

Everything comes from putting a = −g in the constant-acceleration equations.

STEP 1

v = u − g t(A) ★

Put a = −g in v = u + at. One straight line in time — this single equation is the whole velocity story.

STEP 2

y = u t − ½g t²(B)

The same substitution in s = ut + ½at² gives the height measured from the launch point.

STEP 3

v² = u² − 2g y(C) ★

Remove t between (A) and (B). Now speed depends only on height — time has left the equation.

STEP 4

v = 0 ⇒ tup = u/g , ymax = u²/2gapex

The top of the flight is where velocity crosses zero in (A). The acceleration there is still −g.

STEP 5

y = 0 ⇒ t = 2u/g , v = −u(D) ★

Put y = 0 in (B), then substitute back in (A): the flight takes exactly double the up-time, and the ball returns with the same speed it left with. Up and down are mirror images — and mass appears nowhere.

WHERE — u: launch velocity (m/s, up positive) · v: velocity at time t (m/s) · g: 9.80 m/s² · y: height above launch (m) · t: time from launch (s) · ★ marks formula-sheet equations.
FIG 2 — equation (A) drawn: one straight line, through zero at the apex. It crosses zero — it never bends, never bounces.
THE APEX TRAP

At the very top, velocity is zero — acceleration is not. It stays −9.80 m/s² through the turn. Any answer built on "a = 0 at the top" fails before it starts.

04 Special cases

Six ready-made forms of the same three equations. Learn the trigger, not just the formula.

KIN-006

Return-speed symmetry

Speed at any level while coming down equals the speed at that level while going up — mass never enters.

TRIGGER: BACK TO THE LAUNCH LEVEL
KIN-007

Speed–height square root

During a drop, v = √(2g × drop). After a bounce to half the height, the same curve repeats at a smaller scale.

TRIGGER: SPEED AT A HEIGHT, NOT AT A TIME
KIN-008

Tower relation

Total flight = n × time to the top, landing a height H below the launch: 2gH = nu²(n − 2).

TRIGGER: FLIGHT TIME GIVEN AS A MULTIPLE
KIN-009

The v–t line

v = u − gt: one straight line of slope −g, through zero at the apex, negative on the way down (Fig 2).

TRIGGER: ANY VERTICAL-THROW GRAPH
KIN-010

Linear separation

Two bodies, both in the air: y₂ − y₁ = (u₂ − u₁)t — the ½gt² terms cancel, so the gap grows along a straight line.

TRIGGER: TWO BODIES, BOTH IN FLIGHT
KIN-011

The split-fall optimum

A fall of H interrupted at height h: t = √(2(H−h)/g) + √(2h/g), maximum at h/H = 1/2.

05 Examples & problem tackling

Five real exam patterns. For each one: the setup, how to attack, the trap, and the line that finishes it.

KIN-006PYQ 1983-S1Q1

Free-fall symmetry

FIG 3 — same throw speed, different masses, same return speed.
THE SETUP

Two balls of different mass are thrown up with the same speed. Compare their speeds when they come back to the launch level.

HOW TO ATTACK

Look at equations (A) to (D) — mass appears nowhere. Apply the mirror result directly: each ball returns with the speed it left with.

THE TRAP

Feeling that the heavier ball should come back faster. Gravity gives every mass the same acceleration — the feeling is wrong.

THE LINE THAT FINISHES IT

Motion under gravity is mass-independent; speed depends only on height — equal speeds, both balls.

KIN-007 · KIN-009PYQ 2000-S1Q14 · 2017-S1Q21

Reading the graphs

FIG 4 — the two graphs this section keeps asking for.
THE SETUP

Two old favourites in one skill: sketch speed against height for a ball dropped from d that rebounds to d/2, and pick the correct velocity–time graph for a ball thrown straight up.

HOW TO ATTACK

Against height, use (C): v = √(2g(d − h)) while falling, and the same square-root shape at a smaller scale after the bounce. Against time, use (A): one straight line of slope −g crossing zero at the apex.

THE TRAP

Drawing v as a straight line against h — it is a sideways parabola. And picking a v–t curve that bends or never changes sign — the true line crosses zero, it does not bounce off the axis.

THE LINE THAT FINISHES IT

v ∝ √height against height; v = u − gt, straight with slope −g, against time.

KIN-008PYQ 2014-S1Q19

Throw from a tower

FIG 5 — the landing is BELOW the launch, so the displacement enters with a minus sign.
THE SETUP

From a tower of height H you throw a particle straight up. The total flight to the ground is n times the time taken to reach the top. Find the relation between H, u and n.

HOW TO ATTACK

Time to the top is t_up = u/g, so landing happens at t = nu/g. Put this in (B) with the landing displacement y = −H (the base is below the launch). Simplify and you get 2gH = nu²(n − 2).

THE TRAP

Writing y = +H. The particle finishes below the point of throw — with up as positive, the displacement must be −H, otherwise every sign after it goes wrong.

THE LINE THAT FINISHES IT

Up positive, net displacement = −H at the base — then 2gH = nu²(n − 2) comes out in two lines.

KIN-010PYQ 2015-S1Q20

Two stones, one cliff

FIG 6 — gravity cancels from the difference, so the gap grows on a straight line while both fly.
THE SETUP

Two stones are thrown up from the same cliff with different speeds. Sketch their separation against time, keeping in mind that each stone eventually lands.

HOW TO ATTACK

Write (B) for both and subtract: the −½gt² terms cancel, leaving y₂ − y₁ = (u₂ − u₁)t. While both are in the air the gap grows along a straight line; the graph changes its nature the moment the slower stone lands.

THE TRAP

Drawing the separation as a curve during the shared flight. Gravity acts equally on both stones — it cannot bend their difference.

THE LINE THAT FINISHES IT

While both fly, relative acceleration is zero: gap = (u₂ − u₁)t, a straight line — then watch for the first landing.

KIN-011PYQ 1986-S2Q3

The fall that breaks in two

FIG 7 — one fall, two parts: a drop of H − h, then a fresh fall from h.
THE SETUP

A body falls a height H and strikes an incline at height h, where its velocity becomes horizontal. Find h/H so that the time still left to reach the ground is maximum.

HOW TO ATTACK

Break the journey into two parts: a straight drop of H − h, then — velocity now horizontal — a fresh fall from height h. Add the two fall times, t = √(2(H−h)/g) + √(2h/g), and maximise over h.

THE TRAP

Maximising only one part. Raise h and the second fall gets longer while the first gets shorter — only the sum has the answer.

THE LINE THAT FINISHES IT

Differentiate the sum, set it to zero: the symmetric split h/H = 1/2 wins.
